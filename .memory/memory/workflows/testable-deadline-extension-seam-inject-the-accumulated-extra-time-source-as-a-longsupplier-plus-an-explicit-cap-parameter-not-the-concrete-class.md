AssistantLocalAgentRunner.awaitProcessWithDeadlineExtension(Process, Duration timeout, long maxExtensionMillis, LongSupplier accumulatedPendingMillisSupplier) (#3633) takes the extension amount as a LongSupplier and the cap as a plain long parameter, rather than taking the concrete LocalAgentApprovalBridge (which has a hardcoded 10-minute real cap and only advances via real wall-clock Instant.now() calls). The production caller (awaitProcessWithApprovalExtension) supplies bridge::accumulatedPendingMillis and the real MAX_APPROVAL_EXTENSION_SECONDS=600 constant; tests supply a fixed/fake LongSupplier and a tiny fake cap (e.g. 100ms), so a test proving 'abandonment past the cap still kills the run' finishes in ~150ms of real test time instead of needing to actually wait out a 10-minute cap. Why: a deadline/timeout feature with a real-world cap measured in minutes is untestable at that magnitude without either mocking time or parameterizing the magnitude itself -- parameterizing was simpler than introducing a Clock abstraction here since only two numbers actually vary (the extension amount and the cap). How to apply: when adding a capped, dynamically-computed deadline extension to any polling/wait loop, extract the polling loop itself as a package-private method taking the volatile quantity as a supplier/function and the cap as a plain parameter, and keep the production-only pieces (which concrete object supplies the value, what the real cap constant is) in a thin private wrapper that tests don't need to call.
